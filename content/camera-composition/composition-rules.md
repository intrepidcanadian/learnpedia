Composition is where art meets geometry. These aren't rules to follow blindly — they're tools to deploy intentionally.

## Rule of Thirds

Place subjects at intersection points of a 3×3 grid. Creates natural, balanced tension. Prompt: `"subject positioned at rule-of-thirds intersection"` or simply `"rule of thirds composition"`.

## Center Framing

Subject dead center. Creates symmetry, power, confrontation. Prompt: `"centered symmetrical framing"` or `"symmetrical center composition"`.

Center framing conveys formality, authority, or unsettling stillness. It works best when the environment supports the symmetry — hallways, doorways, architectural spaces. Breaking center framing mid-scene (subject shifts off-center) creates a strong visual disruption.

## Leading Lines

Use environmental lines (roads, hallways, fences) to draw the eye toward the subject. Prompt: `"leading lines drawing the eye toward [subject]"` or `"converging perspective lines leading to subject"`.

Leading lines are strongest when they originate from frame edges or corners. Diagonal lines add energy; horizontal lines add calm; vertical lines add structure. In wide aspect ratios (2.39:1), horizontal leading lines get more screen real estate — use them to pull the viewer across the frame.

## Negative Space

Intentionally empty areas around the subject. Creates isolation, breathing room, or tension depending on context. Prompt: `"subject surrounded by negative space"`.

## Foreground / Midground / Background Layering

Depth through layers. A blurred foreground element + sharp subject + soft background = cinematic depth. Prompt: `"layered composition with foreground element, sharp subject in midground, blurred background"`.

## Frame Within a Frame

Use doorways, windows, arches to create a natural frame around your subject. Adds depth and focus. Prompt: `"subject framed through a doorway/window/arch"`.

## Headroom and Look Space

Two often-overlooked framing fundamentals:

- **Headroom** — Space between the top of the subject's head and the top of the frame. Too much feels empty; too little feels claustrophobic. For medium and close-up shots, leave a small sliver of space. Prompt: `"properly framed with natural headroom"`.
- **Look space / lead room** — When a character faces or moves toward one side of the frame, leave more space in the direction they're looking or moving. This feels natural and intentional. Prompt: `"subject positioned with lead room in the direction of movement"`.

These matter most for dialogue, interviews, and character-focused shots. Violating them intentionally (zero headroom, no lead room) creates tension and claustrophobia.

## Composition by Aspect Ratio

Different aspect ratios demand different composition strategies:

| Aspect Ratio | Composition Approach |
|---|---|
| **2.39:1 (Scope)** | Maximize horizontal leading lines and look space. Headroom is limited — use it sparingly. Two-subject framing works naturally side by side. |
| **16:9 (Widescreen)** | The default for most AI video. Rule of thirds works well here. Balanced for both lateral and vertical composition. |
| **4:3 (Academy)** | More vertical space than 16:9. Center framing and frame-within-frame feel natural. Good for portraits and dialogue. |
| **9:16 (Vertical)** | Stack subjects top-to-bottom. Use foreground/background separation instead of lateral composition. Leading lines should be vertical. Negative space above or below the subject creates mood. |
| **1:1 (Square)** | Center framing dominates. Rule of thirds still works but feels less dynamic. Strong for graphic, symmetrical compositions. |

## When Rules Conflict

Composition rules sometimes pull in different directions. When they do:

- **Intent wins.** Decide what the shot communicates first, then pick the rule that serves that intent.
- **One dominant rule per shot.** A shot can use rule of thirds AND leading lines, but one should dominate — the other supports.
- **Breaking rules intentionally** is a composition technique. Zero headroom, subject at frame edge, dead center in an asymmetrical space — all create deliberate tension. The key: break one rule at a time so the audience reads it as intentional, not accidental.
